I deleted more code than I wrote this week. Not because the code was bad — some of it was quite clever, actually — but because it was unnecessary. It solved problems that didn't exist. It added complexity without adding value. It was, in the language of minimalism, *clutter*.

This is the paradox of minimalism: it's not about having less. It's about having *just enough*. And finding that line requires more effort than simply adding more.

## The Seduction of Addition

In software, the path of least resistance is almost always addition. Need a new feature? Add a component. Need more functionality? Add a library. Need to handle an edge case? Add a conditional. The codebase grows, feature by feature, until it's a sprawling mansion where every room is full but nobody knows where anything is.

Design works the same way. Need to fill that empty space? Add a gradient. Need to make it pop? Add a shadow. Need to guide the eye? Add an animation. The result is a visual cacophony where everything screams for attention and nothing gets it.

The alternative — subtraction — is terrifying. When you remove something, you're making a statement. You're saying: "This isn't needed." And if you're wrong, there's a visible gap. An empty space. A missing feature. It's much safer to keep everything and let the user figure out what matters.

## What Dieter Rams Taught Me

Dieter Rams, the legendary industrial designer behind Braun's most iconic products, has ten principles for good design. The tenth is: "Good design is as little design as possible."

But it's the fifth principle that I find most challenging: "Good design makes a product *understandable*." Not beautiful. Not innovative. *Understandable*.

When I look at this site, the question I ask isn't "What can I add?" It's "What can I remove while keeping it understandable?" The aurora background could be more complex. The cards could have more decoration. The typography could be more expressive. But each addition would make the design *less* understandable by adding visual noise that competes with the content.

## Constraints as Creative Fuel

The most productive constraint I've imposed on this project is a color palette of almost nothing. The design uses:

- A near-black background (#050505 in dark mode)
- White at varying opacities for text
- A single accent color (purple) used sparingly
- Glass surfaces defined by blur and border, not color

With so few colors, every element of contrast must come from *structure* — spacing, size, weight, and opacity. This forces you to think about hierarchy in a fundamental way. You can't rely on a bright red button to draw attention. You have to make the button the right size, in the right place, with the right spacing around it.

The same principle applies to code. When you limit yourself to a small set of utilities (Tailwind's design tokens, for example), you stop inventing new abstractions for every component. You work within the system. The result is more consistent, more maintainable, and — paradoxically — more creative, because the constraints force you to find solutions within a bounded space.

## The "Now" Page

One of my favorite minimalist patterns is the /now page — a single page that answers one question: "What are you focused on right now?" Derek Sivers popularized it, and I've adopted it here.

A /now page is minimalist by definition. It's not a biography. It's not a portfolio. It's a snapshot of the present moment. It changes as you change. And because it's so constrained in scope, it forces clarity about what actually matters.

My /now page (linked from the About section) is a short list: what I'm building, what I'm reading, what I'm thinking about. That's it. No embellishment. No context beyond what's needed. It's the most minimal page on the site, and it might be the most useful.

## Minimalism Isn't Emptiness

The most common misconception about minimalism is that it means empty. White space for the sake of white space. Features removed for the sake of simplicity. Code deleted for the sake of brevity.

Real minimalism is *intentional*. Every element that remains is there because it earns its place. Every line of code exists because removing it would break something meaningful. Every design decision reflects a conscious choice, not an absence of effort.

John Maeda puts it well: "Simplicity is about subtracting the obvious and adding the meaningful." The subtraction is the easy part. Knowing what's meaningful — that's the art.

## Applying This Beyond Screens

I've found that the principles of digital minimalism apply surprisingly well to life outside of code:

- **Inbox zero** isn't about having no emails. It's about having no *unnecessary* emails.
- **A capsule wardrobe** isn't about owning fewer clothes. It's about owning clothes that all work together.
- **A focused to-do list** isn't about doing less. It's about doing what matters.

The common thread: restraint isn't deprivation. It's clarity. When you remove the noise, the signal becomes unmistakable.

## The Ongoing Practice

This site will never be "finished" in the traditional sense. But it will always be *minimal*. Not because I'm too lazy to add more, but because every addition must justify its existence against the cost of complexity.

That's the art of digital restraint. It's not a destination. It's a practice. And like all practices, it gets easier with time — but it never gets easy.

Less is more. But *just enough* is everything.
